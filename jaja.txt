This mathematical result is formally known as the **Goldbach-Euler Theorem**. It states that the sum of the reciprocals of the numbers of the form p - 1, where p is a perfect power (excluding 1 and without counting duplicates), is equal to 1.
Here is the rigorous, step-by-step proof.
### **1. Definitions**
 * **Perfect Powers (P):** The set of all integers p that can be expressed as m^n, where m and n are integers such that m \ge 2 and n \ge 2.
   
 * **Non-Perfect Powers (B):** The set of all integers x \ge 2 that *cannot* be expressed as m^n for any integers m, n \ge 2.
   
> **Theorem (Goldbach-Euler):**
> $$ \sum_{p \in P} \frac{1}{p-1} = 1 $$
> 
### **2. Proof**
**Step 1: Evaluate the Infinite Double Sum of All Integers**
Consider the double series over all integers k \ge 2 and all exponents n \ge 2:
$$ S = \sum_{k=2}^{\infty} \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k^n} $$
Because this series converges absolutely, we can evaluate the inner sum directly. For any fixed integer k, the inner sum is an infinite geometric series with the first term \frac{1}{k^2} and a common ratio of \frac{1}{k}:
$$ \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k^n} = \frac{\frac{1}{k^2}}{1 - \frac{1}{k}} = \frac{1}{k(k-1)} = \frac{1}{k-1} - \frac{1}{k} $$
Summing this result over all k \ge 2 yields a telescoping series:
$$ S = \sum_{k=2}^{\infty} \left( \frac{1}{k-1} - \frac{1}{k} \right) $$
$$ S = \left( 1 - \frac{1}{2} \right) + \left( \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{3} \right) + \left( \frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{4} \right) + \dots = 1 $$
Thus, we have established that:
$$ \sum_{k=2}^{\infty} \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k^n} = 1 $$
**Step 2: Partition the Sum into Two Sets**
Every integer k \ge 2 is either a non-perfect power (k \in B) or a perfect power (k \in P). Because the original double series is absolutely convergent, we can cleanly split the sum into these two mutually exclusive sets without altering its value:
$$ 1 = \sum_{x \in B} \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{x^n} + \sum_{p \in P} \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{p^n} $$
**Step 3: Evaluate the Sum over Non-Perfect Powers (B)**
Let us analyze the first term: \sum_{x \in B} \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{x^n}.
By the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic, every perfect power p \in P can be uniquely written as p = x^n, where x is a non-perfect power (x \in B) and the exponent is n \ge 2.
Because this mapping is a strict bijection—meaning each combination of (x, n) corresponds to exactly one perfect power and vice versa—summing \frac{1}{x^n} over all x \in B and n \ge 2 generates the reciprocal of every perfect power exactly once.
Therefore, this sum is simply the sum of the reciprocals of all perfect powers:
$$ \sum_{x \in B} \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{x^n} = \sum_{p \in P} \frac{1}{p} $$
**Step 4: Evaluate the Sum over Perfect Powers (P)**
Now, let us evaluate the second term: \sum_{p \in P} \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{p^n}.
We apply the exact same geometric series logic derived in Step 1, but restricted only to values where p \in P:
$$ \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{p^n} = \frac{1}{p-1} - \frac{1}{p} $$
Substituting this back into the summation over P gives:
$$ \sum_{p \in P} \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{p^n} = \sum_{p \in P} \left( \frac{1}{p-1} - \frac{1}{p} \right) $$
Because the sub-series \sum \frac{1}{p} and \sum \frac{1}{p-1} converge independently (as they are subsets of \sum \frac{1}{n^2}), we can separate them mathematically:
$$ \sum_{p \in P} \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{p^n} = \sum_{p \in P} \frac{1}{p-1} - \sum_{p \in P} \frac{1}{p} $$
**Step 5: Conclusion**
Substitute the derived results from Step 3 and Step 4 back into the partitioned equation established in Step 2:
$$ 1 = \left( \sum_{p \in P} \frac{1}{p} \right) + \left( \sum_{p \in P} \frac{1}{p-1} - \sum_{p \in P} \frac{1}{p} \right) $$
The positive and negative sums of the reciprocals of the perfect powers cleanly cancel each other out:
$$ 1 = \sum_{p \in P} \frac{1}{p-1} $$
**\blacksquare Q.E.D.**
